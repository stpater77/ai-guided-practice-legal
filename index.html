Advanced Technique

Build the other side. Before they build you.

The firm across the table is already using AI. The question isn't whether AI changes litigation — it's whether you're using it to think, or just to type.

The other side is already doing this.

Insurance defense firms were the earliest adopters of AI in litigation. They're using it to draft motions faster, find vulnerabilities in your pleadings, and model your settlement posture. If you're using AI as a typist while they're using it as a strategist, you're not competing — you're bringing a calculator to a chess match.

Motion Practice War Gaming

You draft a motion to compel. Your persona files a protective order the same day — tells you exactly what grounds, what rule, and why it'll probably work. You fix the motion before you file it.

Settlement Negotiation Sparring

Your persona lowballs you. Then tells you why: soft specials, no surgery, IME doctor who'll say degenerative. Tells you what changes the calculus. You walk into negotiation knowing the ceiling before they set the floor.

Deposition Prep From the Other Chair

Your persona tells you what questions it doesn't want you to ask — which means those are exactly the questions you should ask. It shows you where your witness is vulnerable and builds impeachment sequences.

Expert Witness Attack Plans

Your persona maps out the Daubert challenge against your expert before opposing counsel even hires theirs. Qualifications, methodology, reliability — the full attack, so you can bulletproof the disclosure.

Live Demo — What This Looks Like in Practice
You (Plaintiff's Attorney)
I'm filing a motion to compel production of the defendant's cell phone records for the 24 hours surrounding the accident.
Your Opposing Counsel Persona
I'm filing a protective order the same day. Here's my argument: the request is overbroad — you're asking for 24 hours of records when the collision spanned 30 seconds. I'm invoking Florida's right to privacy, Article I Section 23. I'm also arguing the records include privileged communications with my client, their spouse, and their therapist. I'll propose narrowed production — 60 minutes surrounding impact, with redactions — and the judge will probably give me that. And I'm asking for sanctions under Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.380 for filing an overbroad request. Your move.
You
Good catch. Let me revise the meet-and-confer. What if I narrow to 60 minutes around impact?
Your Opposing Counsel Persona
I'd still fight it, but I'd lose on the narrowing. My next move: I produce the records, then move in limine to exclude any reference to cell phone usage at trial unless you can prove she was actively using the phone at the moment of impact. Burden's on you, not me. Also — don't ask the defendant about his cell phone in deposition. Not yet. If you lock in his testimony that he wasn't on his phone, you can't impeach him later with the records you're about to get. Ask what he was doing in the 60 seconds before impact. Let him paint himself into a corner.

FAQ

Common questions

Do I need any coding experience?
Absolutely not. If you can use a web browser and type an email, you can do everything I teach. That's not a marketing line — it's the truth. Every workflow uses no-code tools or simple AI interfaces.
Is my client data safe?
Confidentiality is non-negotiable in legal. I'll show you which tools have enterprise-grade security, which ones are safe for client work, and which to avoid. Every workflow we build is designed around attorney-client privilege and data protection from the start — not as an afterthought.
What about AI hallucinations in legal research?
This is exactly why generic AI courses don't work for lawyers. I teach you how to verify outputs, build verification workflows, and use AI as a first-draft tool (not a final authority). You always review before anything leaves your desk — just like with a junior associate's work product.
Is using AI for legal work ethically permissible?
Yes — and most state bars have now issued guidance confirming it. The key is using it responsibly: understanding the tools, supervising the output, and maintaining your professional judgment. I'll walk you through the current ABA and state-specific ethics guidance so you can use AI with confidence.
How is this different from a $29 Udemy course?
Udemy courses teach you about AI. I teach you how to use AI in YOUR practice. We work on your actual documents, your actual workflows, your actual cases. You leave every session with something that works — not just knowledge, but a system your firm can use tomorrow.
What if I'm already using some AI tools?
Great — we'll audit what you're using, optimize your prompts and workflows, and identify gaps. Most attorneys I work with are using ChatGPT for basic tasks but have no idea what else is possible. That's where the real time savings are — in the workflows, not the individual prompts.
What practice areas do you cover?
The fundamentals apply across all practice areas. I've worked with attorneys in corporate, litigation, family, real estate, IP, employment, and criminal defense. The AI tools are the same — what changes is how we apply them to your specific documents, research needs, and client communication.
